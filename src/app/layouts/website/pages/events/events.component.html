Un ordine delle cose is a musical reading of excerpts from L’ordine delle cose, a collection of philosophical essays that reveal to humankind the world of living beings in its absence, inviting us to discover, precisely, the order of things. The volume, published in 1958, is the work of Jacques Brosse, a psychoanalyst, and an expert in botany and religions. Renato Grieco composed the musical interludes in February 2024, while reading and rereading this gem. Science and truth blend with imagination and the ingenuousness of a gaze attentive to microscopic details.

Renato Grieco is a composer and musician from Naples, south Italy. Grieco has been performing internationally since 2012, both as a solo artist (under the moniker kNN) and in numerous collaborations as musician, performer and sound dramaturge. Grieco's music originates in musique concrète craftsmanship, culminating in orchestrations where period instruments, imaginary lutherie, and sculptural rooms coexist. Speculative folklore, vocal architecture, and hauntology are the kernel of his practice.

curve25519 is a work that focuses on the intrinsic quality of secrecy that arises in the miracle of the sublimation of the verbal into a non-public, intimate, private communication. The name of this work is that of a particular elliptic curve on the finite field defined by the pseudo-Mersenne prime number 2^255 - 19, hence the “25519” in the name. It implements a cryptography used to establish a shared secret through an insecure channel. The performance is based on 'c2oC' (computer2organs Communication system), in which the computer mediates communication between two pre-MIDI electric organs electronically hacked to interact with each other. The harmonic ornamental motifs of the organs located in the performative space intertwine with digitally synthesized sounds and the natural harmonics of the voice. This system was first presented to the public in the 2019 electroacoustic work “the neat sensation of our separation,” hosted in a quadraphonic version by the Chiostro di San Michele in Fano, the Cimitero Monumentale della Certosa in Bologna, and the Conservatorio G.B. Martini.

Martenot is the solo moniker of Simone Gottardi, an experimental musician and sound artist based in Turin. His compositions, exclusively computer-generated, are founded on the use of a minimalist vocabulary and a digital synthesis apparatus shaped by strict constraints. This approach establishes a precise aesthetic direction, characterized by the dichotomous alternation of claustrophobic patterns, which evoke the rigid molecular structures of plastic materials, in contrast to freer and more irregular sections in which the musical fabric dissolves and opens up to airy and rarefied scenarios.

In 2022, he launched his curatorial project Neanche Per Sogno, releasing his debut EP Sfilate Spaziali, followed by a series of live performances and the release of Faceshopping RMX in July 2023. From the Beach Boys to Ariel Pink, from Daniel Johnston to the Microphones/Mount Eerie, the so-called lo-fi—short for “low fidelity”—has now become a major chapter in the history of indie music. But what fascinates us about these grainy, home-recorded sounds, which drown exquisite pop melodies in a tangle of hiss and unintended reverberations? And how can lo-fi help us understand our relationship with the instruments of power through technology, so that we might hack them to shape our most intimate fantasies? Part music essay, part private diary, part refined experiment in applied philosophy, Bassa Fedeltà drags us into the dreamlike realms of “bedroom music” par excellence, making Brian Wilson converse with Félix Guattari, Perfume Genius with Mark Fisher, black metal with Paolo Virno, transforming lo-fi musicians into cyberpunk explorers of DIY recording and revealing how these sounds—upending the hi-tech dogma—tell us about ourselves, our lives under capitalism, and the strange ways we grapple with pain, madness, and beauty.

Translation into Italian by Tommaso Garavaglia.

SISTERS WITH TRANSISTORS is the untold story of electronic music’s female pioneers, remarkable composers who embraced machines and their liberating technologies to utterly transform how we produce and listen to music today.

The remarkable world of electronic music has been constantly evolving since its inception in the early 20th century. From the unlimited possibilities of digital synthesisers, to the recent analog recording revival; from the impossibly perfect pitch of Auto-Tune, to the democratisation of music making for a generation of 'bedroom’ producers, each of these extraordinary developments is directly connected to the work and artistry of the women featured in “Sisters with Transistors”.

Clara Rockmore, Daphne Oram, Bebe Barron, Delia Derbyshire, Maryanne Amacher, Pauline Oliveros, Wendy Carlos, Eliane Radigue, Suzanne Ciani, and Laurie Spiegel are among the greatest pioneers of modern sound and we contin ue to feel their influence yet most people have never heard of them.

Deep Listening is a practice created by Pauline Oliveros, an American queer composer, improviser, and performer, which explores the difference between the involuntary nature of hearing and the voluntary, selective nature of listening. In her book Deep Listening: A Composer's Sound Practice, she defines deep listening as learning to expand one’s perception of sounds to include the entire space-time continuum of sound, embracing as much of its vastness and complexity as possible.

Practically, deep listening involves exercises in bodily proprioception and guided listening to everyday sounds. In this workshop, listening is explored as a poetic tool to expand the boundaries of reality. Starting from Pauline Oliveros’ deep listening practice, participants are guided to rediscover the imaginative potential of listening, allowing inner sounds to reveal new, unexplored landscapes.

Diana Lola Posani is a sound artist, vocal performer, and certified Deep Listening facilitator through the Deep Listening Foundation. She performs internationally, writes for A Row of Trees, the journal of the Sonic Art Research Unit (SARU) at Oxford Brookes University, and debuted in 2022 on Fango Radio with the podcast Kaikokaipuu. Her current research focuses on the shared space between sound and poetic imagination through interdisciplinary works and sound poetry. In March 2023, she published her Italian translation of Pauline Oliveros’ Deep Listening: A Composer's Sound Practice with Timeo.
